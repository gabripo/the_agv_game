Executive The Business Case for Sensor Fusion

Imagine walking through a pitch-black warehouse. You count your steps (odometry), but each step introduces drift — after 50 meters, you could be several meters off course. Every time you reach out and touch a wall (sensor update), you correct your mental map.

The Extended Kalman Filter does exactly this for autonomous vehicles: it continuously weighs the robot's internal motion estimate against external sensor readings. When tuned correctly, it ensures the robot always knows where it is — even when the floor is slippery or visual landmarks disappear.

Business outcome: Reliable navigation without expensive high-precision sensors. Lower costs, higher uptime, safer operations.

Engineer The Predict-Update Loop & Covariance Tuning

The EKF operates in two phases per timestep:

1. Predict (Motion Model): The state x is advanced via a non-linear kinematic function f(x, u). The covariance P grows because the motion model has uncertainty — this is encoded in the Process Noise matrix Q.

2. Update (Sensor Fusion): A noisy measurement z arrives. The filter computes the Kalman Gain K = P·Hᵀ(H·P·Hᵀ + R)⁻¹, which optimally weights the prediction vs. the measurement. The Measurement Noise matrix R determines how much the filter trusts the sensor.

The Q and R matrices are your confidence dials:

— Low Q + high R = "trust the motion model, ignore noisy sensors"
— High Q + low R = "trust the sensors, the motion model is unreliable"

A balanced tuning (Q ≈ R in the right units) allows the filter to dynamically adapt — trusting the model when sensors fail, and trusting sensors when the model drifts.

Specialist EKF Mathematics & Jacobian Linearization

State Vector (4 DOF): planar pose + velocity

$$ \mathbf{x}_k = \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \\ \theta \\ v \end{bmatrix} $$

Non-Linear Motion Model

$$ \mathbf{x}_{k+1} = f(\mathbf{x}_k, \mathbf{u}_k) = \begin{bmatrix} x_k + v_k \cos(\theta_k) \Delta t \\ y_k + v_k \sin(\theta_k) \Delta t \\ \theta_k + \dot{\theta} \Delta t \\ v_k + \dot{v} \Delta t \end{bmatrix} $$

Jacobian of f w.r.t. state (linearization point: current estimate)

$$ \mathbf{F}_k = \frac{\partial f}{\partial \mathbf{x}} \bigg|_{\mathbf{x}_{k-1}, \mathbf{u}_k} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & -v_k \sin(\theta_k) \Delta t & \cos(\theta_k) \Delta t \\ 0 & 1 & v_k \cos(\theta_k) \Delta t & \sin(\theta_k) \Delta t \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} $$

Predict Step

$$ \mathbf{x}_k^- = f(\mathbf{x}_{k-1}, \mathbf{u}_k) $$
$$ \mathbf{P}_k^- = \mathbf{F}_k \mathbf{P}_{k-1} \mathbf{F}_k^\mathsf{T} + \mathbf{Q} $$

Measurement Model (range & bearing to landmark at (lx, ly))

$$ \mathbf{z}_k = h(\mathbf{x}_k) = \begin{bmatrix} \sqrt{(l_x - x_k)^2 + (l_y - y_k)^2} \\ \text{atan2}(l_y - y_k,\; l_x - x_k) - \theta_k \end{bmatrix} $$

Jacobian of h w.r.t. state

$$ \mathbf{H}_k = \frac{\partial h}{\partial \mathbf{x}} \bigg|_{\mathbf{x}_k^-} = \begin{bmatrix} -\frac{\Delta x}{d} & -\frac{\Delta y}{d} & 0 & 0 \\ \frac{\Delta y}{d^2} & -\frac{\Delta x}{d^2} & -1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} $$

where Δx = lx − x, Δy = ly − y, d = √(Δx² + Δy²)

Kalman Gain & Update Step

$$ \mathbf{K}_k = \mathbf{P}_k^- \mathbf{H}_k^\mathsf{T} \left( \mathbf{H}_k \mathbf{P}_k^- \mathbf{H}_k^\mathsf{T} + \mathbf{R} \right)^{-1} $$
$$ \mathbf{x}_k^+ = \mathbf{x}_k^- + \mathbf{K}_k \left( \mathbf{z}_k - h(\mathbf{x}_k^-) \right) $$
$$ \mathbf{P}_k^+ = \left( \mathbf{I} - \mathbf{K}_k \mathbf{H}_k \right) \mathbf{P}_k^- $$

Key Insight: Because both f and h are non-linear, we linearize them via their Jacobians at each timestep — this is what makes the filter Extended. The Jacobians capture the local slope of the non-linear functions, enabling approximately optimal fusion of non-linear models and measurements.
